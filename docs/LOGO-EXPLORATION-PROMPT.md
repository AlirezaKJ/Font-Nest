# FontNest Logo Exploration Prompt

Use this prompt with an AI design system that can generate editable vector artwork. If the tool only generates raster images, ask it to follow the fallback delivery instructions while preserving flat vector construction.

## Copy-paste prompt

```text
Act as a senior identity designer specializing in desktop creative tools, typography products, and highly scalable app icons.

Create a broad logo exploration for a desktop application named “FontNest.” Produce exactly 36 genuinely distinct vector logo concepts—not minor variations, recolors, rotations, or stroke changes of the same idea.

PRODUCT CONTEXT

FontNest is a lightweight professional desktop application for inspecting and managing fonts installed on a computer. Its primary users are designers, typographers, frontend engineers, and other typography-focused professionals. The product helps users browse and search font families, preview faces and weights, inspect glyphs and variable axes, identify duplicate or conflicting font files, and safely manage fonts installed through the application.

BRAND CHARACTER

- precise
- calm
- discerning
- professional and approachable
- expert without feeling exclusive
- restrained enough that the user’s fonts remain the expressive focus

CORE BRAND IDEA

Interpret “nest” as a deliberate structure that gathers, protects, and organizes many distinct forms. Connect this with typography’s natural systems: families, faces, weights, glyphs, counters, baselines, files, and collections. The mark should communicate organization and inspection—not birds, nature, or domestic coziness.

CONCEPT TERRITORIES

Create six concepts in each of these six territories, for 36 concepts total:

1. FN monograms: combine F and N through negative space, shared stems, counters, ligatures, folding, or modular geometry.
2. Typographic anatomy: abstract ideas from glyph counters, baselines, type specimens, movable type, letterform construction, or font-family relationships.
3. Organized nest: express gathering, containment, protection, or nested structures without drawing a literal bird nest.
4. Catalogue system: reduce font families, file stacks, collections, grids, sorting, or grouped faces into a distinctive symbol.
5. Inspection and discovery: suggest close inspection, comparison, focus, or revealing hidden font details without using a generic standalone magnifying-glass icon.
6. Hybrid and wildcard: fuse two meaningful territories above or propose an unexpected concept rooted in FontNest’s actual product behavior.

DESIGN REQUIREMENTS

- Prioritize an ownable app-icon symbol; it must not depend on the wordmark to be recognized.
- Every concept must have a clear one-sentence rationale connecting the geometry to FontNest.
- Keep every mark simple, memorable, balanced, and reducible to clean SVG paths.
- Design for excellent recognition at 16 px, 24 px, 32 px, 64 px, and 256 px.
- Use crisp silhouette, deliberate negative space, and strong internal proportions.
- Ensure every mark works as a single solid color with no loss of meaning.
- Use a consistent optical weight across the exploration, but let the underlying constructions differ substantially.
- Prefer geometric reduction and typographic intelligence over illustration.
- Avoid resemblance to existing font software, technology brands, or famous monograms.
- Do not finalize brand colors yet. Explore entirely in black on white, with a small white-on-black reversal test for each concept.

ABSOLUTE AVOIDS

- no literal birds, eggs, feathers, branches, houses, baskets, or hand-drawn twig nests
- no cozy cottage, rustic, craft-store, or children’s-brand styling
- no generic SaaS sparkles, orbits, blobs, hexagons, shields, or gradient loops
- no generic magnifying glass, folder, file, or download icon used by itself
- no “Aa” tile as the complete idea unless transformed into a truly distinctive construction
- no gradients, shadows, glass effects, 3D, bevels, textures, or mockups during concept exploration
- no fake heraldry, mascots, decorative crests, or ornamental display lettering
- no copied or closely imitated real-world logos
- no concepts that differ only through rotation, corner radius, line weight, or color

VECTOR CONSTRUCTION

- Deliver real editable vectors using closed SVG paths.
- Expand strokes to shapes and remove hidden raster content.
- Use a clean, consistent viewBox and minimal path count.
- Avoid masks and clipping paths unless they are essential to the concept.
- Align geometry to a rational construction grid while applying optical corrections where needed.
- Make the icon safe for Windows, macOS, and Linux app-icon containers, including rounded-square crops without forcing every mark into a rounded-square badge.

DELIVERABLES

1. Three high-resolution contact sheets with 12 concepts per sheet, arranged in a strict 4 × 3 grid with generous spacing.
2. Concepts labelled FN-01 through FN-36 using plain neutral captions outside the artwork.
3. Each tile shows the primary black-on-white mark, a small reversed white-on-black test, and nothing decorative.
4. A separate editable SVG file for every concept, named fontnest-FN-01.svg through fontnest-FN-36.svg.
5. A short rationale for every concept and the territory it belongs to.
6. A final comparison table scoring every concept from 1–5 for distinctiveness, relevance, small-size clarity, balance, and app-icon suitability.
7. Identify the strongest six concepts, but do not combine or refine them yet. Preserve the full breadth of the exploration for human review.

If the current environment cannot output editable SVG files, render the three contact sheets as clean, high-resolution, flat vector-style artwork and provide exact reconstruction notes for every concept. Do not pretend raster artwork is an SVG.

The result should feel like the first exploration round from a serious identity studio: broad, disciplined, typography-aware, strategically grounded, and ready for a human designer to shortlist.
```

## Recommended workflow

Generate the 36 monochrome concepts first. Shortlist six only after reviewing them at app-icon size, then explore construction refinements, wordmarks, and color as separate rounds. This keeps early logo evaluation focused on meaning and silhouette rather than presentation effects.
